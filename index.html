Observation as a Training Evaluation Method

📖 Learning Content

Many HR staff reach for a post-training survey by default. But a survey only tells you how people felt about training — it cannot show you whether they did anything differently back on the job. This section introduces observation: one of the most direct ways to evaluate whether training actually changed workplace behaviour. It sits at Kirkpatrick Level 3 — Behaviour, and it is the method to use when you need real evidence, not just reactions.

What is Workplace Observation?
Observation means systematically watching a learner perform a task in their real or simulated work environment — after training has occurred — to see whether their behaviour has changed. There are two types:

  • Structured observation — uses a pre-designed checklist. The observer knows exactly what behaviours to look for before they walk in. This is the most common and reliable approach for L&D evaluation.
  • Unstructured observation — open-ended field notes with no fixed criteria. More flexible, but harder to compare across observers or time points.

Observation works best for skills-based training: safety procedures, customer service, clinical practice, or any situation where how someone does something matters — not just whether they can recall the theory.

StrengthsShows real behaviour — not self-reported recall. Rich, contextual data about how and why someone acts. Works well for procedural and soft-skills training. Provides strong Level 3 evidence.
LimitationsHawthorne Effect — people behave differently when watched. Time-intensive and difficult to scale. Requires trained, objective observers. Can feel intrusive if not managed carefully.
Kirkpatrick LevelLevel 3 — Behaviour: evaluates whether training changed how employees actually perform on the job.
The Hawthorne EffectPeople tend to change their behaviour simply because they know they are being observed — not because of the training. To reduce this, conduct multiple unannounced visits over time or embed observation into regular supervision.

Example: Customer Service Observation Checklist

Observable BehaviourRating
Agent greets caller by name within 30 secondsDid / Did not / Partially
Agent uses at least one empathy statement during the callDid / Did not / Partially
Agent confirms resolution before ending the callDid / Did not / Partially

Sources: Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick, 2016 CIPD, 2023 CIPD, 2024

Using Questionnaires to Evaluate Training Effectiveness

📖 Learning Content

Questionnaires are one of the most commonly used tools in workplace learning evaluations. They allow HR professionals and L&D staff to gather feedback from learners, identify strengths and weaknesses in training programmes, and assess training effectiveness. They are simple, flexible tools used to measure learner satisfaction, understanding, and areas for improvement.

Target Audience: This section is aimed at HR coordinators and junior L&D employees responsible for workplace training assessments.

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the role of questionnaires in training evaluation.
  • Differentiate between reaction-based and learning-based questions.
  • Create effective evaluation questions.

Why Training Evaluation Matters
Proper evaluation of training justifies organisational investments and identifies areas for improvement. It also ensures that employee development aligns with organisational goals.

Kirkpatrick's Evaluation Model
This section uses Kirkpatrick's framework for evaluating training effectiveness by measuring learner reactions and acquired knowledge. It focuses on two levels: Level 1 (Reaction) — gathering learner feedback, measuring satisfaction, engagement, and overall perception of the training; and Level 2 (Learning) — determining whether learners have acquired the intended knowledge and skills through the training programme. Questionnaires are commonly used to evaluate both levels.

Types of Questionnaire Questions

Reaction Questions (Level 1) Gauge satisfaction and engagement with training. e.g. "Was the training content clear?" / "Was the training relevant to your job role?" / "Did the training meet your expectations?"
Learning Questions (Level 2) Assess knowledge and skills acquired. e.g. "What is one concept you learned?" / "How would you apply this skill at work?" / "Which evaluation method measures performance improvement best?"
Effective Questionnaire Characteristics Clear, short, focused, and aligned with learning outcomes · Includes both rating scale and open-ended questions
Common Mistakes Focusing solely on satisfaction rather than learning outcomes · Vague questioning and leading questions reduce data quality and effectiveness

Sources: Kirkpatrick, 2016 Cloke, 2023 CIPD ATD

Feedback Forms as a Training Evaluation Tool

📖 Learning Content

Most HR coordinators are skilled at organising training: booking sessions, managing attendance, and sending reminders. But evaluation is often an afterthought — a quick satisfaction form that tells you whether people enjoyed the session, not whether anything actually changed.

This section introduces you to Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Evaluation Model, a practical framework used by L&D professionals worldwide, and focuses on how to design meaningful feedback and evaluation tools that give you real evidence that your training worked.

Who this is for: You coordinate and run training programmes at your organisation, but when your manager asks "did that training actually work?", do you have a confident answer?

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Identify the four levels of Kirkpatrick's Evaluation Model and what each level measures
  • Distinguish between a feedback form that only measures reaction and one that also captures evidence of learning
  • Design at least two evaluation questions that go beyond satisfaction to measure whether learning actually occurred

Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Evaluation Model
Developed by Donald Kirkpatrick, this model gives you a structured way to evaluate training at four increasingly meaningful levels. As an HR coordinator, you are most likely measuring Level 1 (reaction) only. This section focuses on how to also capture Level 2 (learning) evidence — because together they give you a much stronger picture of training effectiveness.

Level 1 — ReactionDid learners find the training useful and engaging? Measures satisfaction and perception.
Level 2 — LearningDid they actually gain knowledge or skills? Measures what changed in their understanding.
Level 3 — BehaviourAre they applying it back on the job? Measures real workplace behaviour change.
Level 4 — ResultsDid it impact the organisation's goals? Measures broader business outcomes.

Weak vs Strong Feedback Forms
Most organisations use a basic end-of-training form. Here is the difference:

✗ Weak Form (Reaction only)✓ Strong Form (Reaction + Learning)
"Rate the trainer out of 5""How clearly was the content explained?" (1–5)
"Did you enjoy today's session?""What is one thing you will do differently at work?"
"Would you recommend this training?""Describe the main idea in your own words"

The strong form gives you data you can actually analyse and act on. The weak form only tells you if people were happy — which is useful but nowhere near enough.

⚠️ The problem: Collecting a 5-star satisfaction rating after training tells you whether people enjoyed it. It does not tell you whether they learned anything or changed their behaviour at work. These are very different things.

For additional resources, visit: kirkpatrickpartners.com or search: Setyadi et al. (2024). Evaluating training effectiveness using Kirkpatrick's Model. Conference On Accounting And Business, p. 128.

Sources: Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick, 2016 Setyadi et al., 2024 CIPD

Using Interviews to Measure Training Effectiveness

📖 Learning Content

Target Audience: HR Coordinators, Workplace Trainers, Team Leaders, and L&D Staff. Many HR coordinators collect training feedback via simple surveys, but struggle to gather deeper insights into behavioural change and learning transfer. This section shows how interviews can be used to provide more meaningful evaluation of training effectiveness.

Learning Outcome: At the end of this section, learners will be able to develop 3–4 well-formed interview questions to assess the outcome of workplace training and the behavioural change of employees.

Linked to Kirkpatrick's Model
Training evaluation measures programme success — measuring learner reaction, understanding, and application in the real world. Interviews are especially powerful for evaluating Level 3 — Behaviour: do employees use new skills at work?

What Are Evaluation Interviews?A formal discussion method for collecting in-depth qualitative feedback from employees following workplace training — showing what surveys cannot.
Deeper InsightsUnderstand the 'why' behind learner responses, not just the 'what'. Richer responses than simple rating scales or tick-box surveys.
Behavioural EvidenceDetermine if and how employees apply new skills at work. Identifies emotional reactions and attitudes that surveys miss.
AdvantagesProvides detailed, nuanced responses · Allows clarification of ambiguous answers · Better understanding of behavioural change

Types of Interviews Used in Training Evaluation

StructuredFixed, predetermined questions. Easier to compare answers across employees. Best for: standardised evaluation with large groups.
Semi-Structured ★ RecommendedMix of set and open questions. Best balance of consistency and depth. Best for: training evaluation in most workplace settings.
UnstructuredOpen, conversational approach. Maximum depth and flexibility. Best for: exploratory or pilot feedback.
LimitationsTakes a long time to conduct and analyse · Hard to compare at scale · Potential for interviewer bias · Requires good communication skills

Adult Learning Principles Applied

  • Andragogy: Adults prefer practical workplace examples. This section uses real HR scenarios so learners connect theory to their daily roles.
  • Constructive Alignment: Outcomes, activities, and checks are all aligned — learn interview types → design questions → validate via learning check.
  • Experiential Learning: Learners apply concepts to authentic workplace scenarios, building confidence through doing rather than passive reading.

Key Takeaway: Interviews give training evaluation a human voice. When organisations move beyond tick-box surveys, they uncover the real story of whether training has changed how employees think, act, and perform on the job.

Sources: Bahl, Kiran & Sharma, 2024 ATD — Kirkpatrick Level 3 HBR — Why Leadership Training Fails

Quizzes and Knowledge Checks as Evaluation Tools

📖 Learning Content

This module focuses on applying knowledge checks to evaluate whether training has been effective in achieving its intended outcomes. Rather than focusing on memorisation or simple recall, the emphasis is on applying evaluation concepts to realistic workplace situations. This reflects how organisations actually assess the success of training interventions, where evidence of impact is more important than participant opinions alone.

A key framework used in this module is the Kirkpatrick Model of Evaluation, which is one of the most widely used approaches in Learning and Development. It outlines four levels of training evaluation: Reaction (how participants feel about the training), Learning (what knowledge or skills they have gained), Behaviour (whether they apply learning in the workplace), and Results (the overall impact on organisational performance). Understanding these levels is essential for evaluating training effectiveness in a structured and meaningful way.

In many organisations, evaluation often stops at the reaction or learning level, such as asking participants whether they enjoyed the session or testing them with short quizzes immediately after training. While this information is useful, it does not provide a complete picture of training effectiveness. This module encourages you to go further by focusing on whether learning is transferred into real workplace behaviour, which is a more accurate indicator of training success.

Through a series of scenario-based knowledge checks, you will be asked to identify appropriate evaluation methods and match workplace situations to the correct Kirkpatrick level. These activities are designed to help you think like a Learning and Development practitioner by interpreting evidence, making judgments, and selecting suitable evaluation approaches. By the end of this module, you should be able to confidently apply the Kirkpatrick Model when assessing training effectiveness in practical settings.

  • Pre/post testing compares knowledge before and after to quantify learning gain
  • Scenario-based MCQs test decision-making, not just memorisation
  • Short written justifications reveal depth of understanding beyond a correct tick
  • Digital platforms (Google Forms, Moodle Quiz) enable automatic scoring and instant feedback
Best used forMeasuring knowledge or skill acquisition immediately after training. Works alongside other methods for a complete picture.
Kirkpatrick LevelLevel 2 — Learning: did the training change what learners know or can do?
Key StrengthObjective, scalable, and provides immediate data on whether learning goals were met.
Key LimitationCannot measure actual behaviour change on the job. A correct answer does not guarantee on-the-job application.

Sources: ATD — Assessment Design CIPD Kirkpatrick, 2016
